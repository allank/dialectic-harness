# Office Footprint Review — Background Notes

We've been renewing our downtown lease on autopilot for the past three years, but it's worth revisiting given how much the market has shifted since 2020. U.S. office vacancy rates climbed past the previous national peak set during the 2008 financial crisis sometime in 2023, driven largely by a mix of expiring leases, downsizing, and sustained hybrid-work patterns among white-collar employers. That's a structural shift, not a temporary dip, and it's changed our leverage in lease negotiations considerably.

Attendance data backs this up. Badge-swipe tracking services that monitor office entry across major U.S. metros have shown in-person attendance plateauing at roughly half of pre-pandemic levels for well over a year now, with only modest upward movement on Tuesdays through Thursdays and a sharp drop-off around Mondays and Fridays. Whatever the long-term equilibrium turns out to be, it's clearly not a full return to five-day-a-week attendance.

That said, the pressure to mandate more in-office days isn't going away, and it's worth flagging the tradeoff before anyone pushes hard in that direction: most companies that have rolled out strict return-to-office mandates have seen a measurable drop in employee satisfaction scores within the first quarter after the policy change, and that dip tends to show up in attrition data shortly after. If we're going to negotiate the lease down, we should pair it with a hybrid policy we're actually prepared to defend, not one we plan to walk back the moment the market tightens.

Worth noting: a 2022 Stanford study led by Nicholas Bloom found that fully remote employees were on average 23% more productive than their in-office counterparts, once commute time and office distractions were factored in. If that holds, it strengthens the case for a smaller footprint rather than a larger one — we'd be paying for square footage that isn't buying us the output gains leadership assumes it is.
